THE HARMATTAN LETTERS

A Novel

Chapter One — Dust on the Verandah

When the harmattan came down from the north that December, Ama Serwaa was
seventeen and certain of two things: that she would never leave Bekwai, and that
her grandmother's stories were lies dressed up as history. Both certainties would
not survive the dry season.

The old woman, Nana Afua, sat each evening on the cracked verandah with a tin of
groundnuts and a memory she refused to finish. "Your great-grandfather walked to
the coast," she would begin, and then she would stop, as if the rest of the
sentence cost more than she was willing to spend. Ama had learned not to ask. In
Bekwai, the unfinished sentence was its own kind of inheritance.

It was the schoolteacher, Mr. Boateng, who first put the idea in her head that a
girl from a cocoa town might write things down. He lent her a blue exercise book
and told her, in the careful English he saved for promising students, that every
family in Ghana was carrying a history nobody had bothered to record. "The
British wrote our story for us," he said. "It is time we corrected the spelling."

So Ama began to write. She wrote about the price of cocoa and the men who left for
the mines. She wrote about her mother's silence and her father's empty chair. And
slowly, sentence by unfinished sentence, she began to pull her grandmother's story
out of the dust — a story of a journey to the coast in 1948, of riots she had only
heard named in whispers, and of a name the family had agreed, long ago, never to
say aloud again.

This is the story of that exercise book, and of what it cost to fill it.
