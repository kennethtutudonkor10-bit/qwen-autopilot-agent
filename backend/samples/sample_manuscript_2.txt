THE TROTRO MILLIONAIRE

How I Built a Fortune from a Single Kiosk in Kaneshie

Introduction — The Coin in the Gutter

The first cedi I ever saved, I found in a gutter outside Kaneshie market. I was
eleven. It was wet, it smelled of the drain, and my mother told me to throw it
away. Instead I washed it, dried it in the sun on our windowsill, and hid it
inside a Milo tin under my mattress. That coin is still in that tin today —
except now the tin sits in a glass case in the lobby of a company that turns over
more money in an afternoon than my family saw in a decade. This book is the story
of everything that happened between that gutter and that glass case, and I promise
you: none of it required luck, a rich uncle, or a degree from abroad.

For most of my life, people told me that money was for other people — for the
"connected," for the returnees with foreign accents, for the families who already
had it. I believed them until I was twenty-three, broke, and sleeping in the back
of the very kiosk I would later turn into my first shop. That night, counting
seven cedis and forty pesewas in coins, I made myself a promise that changed
everything: I would stop waiting for money to be given to me, and I would start
building the machine that makes it.

This is not a book of theory. I have never read a business textbook in my life. I
learned economics from selling sachet water in traffic, negotiation from my
grandmother haggling over tomatoes, and patience from watching a mango tree take
seven years to bear. What I am going to give you in these pages is not motivation
— motivation fades by lunchtime. I am going to give you a system: the same nine
habits, five rules, and three hard decisions that took me from that gutter coin to
financial freedom, and that I have since taught to market women, trotro drivers,
teachers, and university dropouts across Ghana who are now quietly wealthy.

If you are reading this in a cramped room, on a borrowed phone, wondering whether
people like us are simply not meant to be rich — I wrote this book for you. I was
you. Turn the page, and let me show you exactly how the machine is built, one
cedi at a time.
