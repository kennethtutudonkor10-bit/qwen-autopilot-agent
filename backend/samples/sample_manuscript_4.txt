KWAKU ANANSE AND THE POT OF WISDOM

A Story for Curious Children

Retold for young readers, ages 6 to 10

Once, a very long time ago, when the forests of Ghana were younger and the animals
still shared their secrets, all the wisdom in the world did not belong to everyone.
It belonged to one clever, tricky, eight-legged fellow named Kwaku Ananse the
spider.

Ananse had gathered wisdom the way a greedy child gathers mangoes — quietly, and
all for himself. He collected clever ideas from the wind, sharp answers from the
river, and good sense from the old baobab tree, and he stuffed every last bit into
a great clay pot. When the pot was full, he pressed the lid down tight and said to
himself, "Now I am the wisest creature in all the land, and I shall keep it that
way!"

But a secret kept alone is a heavy thing to carry. Ananse began to worry that
someone might steal his pot while he slept. So he decided to hide it at the very
top of the tallest odum tree in the forest, where no one could ever reach it.

He tied the heavy pot to the front of his belly with a strong vine and began to
climb. But — oh dear! — the pot kept bumping against the tree trunk and getting in
his way. He climbed and slipped, climbed and slipped, and did not rise even as high
as a goat can jump.

Down below, his small son Ntikuma had been watching all along. "Father," the little
one called up gently, "if you tied the pot to your back instead of your belly, you
could climb much more easily."

Ananse stopped. A small child had just given him an idea — a wise idea — and it was
not even in his pot! And in that moment the tricky spider learned something that
changed everything...
